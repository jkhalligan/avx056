Texas Probate Process\n<\/h2>\t\t<\/div>\n\t\t\t\t<\/div>\n\t\t\t\t
\n\t\t\t\t
\n\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t
\n\t\t\t\t

When a person dies and leaves property that has not been transferred to another person through a Trust, joint ownership with a right of survivorship, or direct payments to beneficiaries, in Texas that property will be distributed through probate.<\/p>

Probate is the process in which a court legally recognizes a person\u2019s death and oversees the payment of a deceased person\u2019s debts and the distribution of his or her assets. The court\u2019s role is to facilitate this process and protect, when necessary, the interests of all creditors and beneficiaries of the estate. The role of the Texas probate court and all persons hired by the court to facilitate this process is known as probate administration.<\/p>

The general rule in Texas is that the executor has four years from the date of death of the person who drafted the will to file for probate. If the executor does not file the will within that time period, the laws of intestacy will govern how the estate\u2019s assets are distributed.<\/p>\t\t\t\t\t<\/div>\n\t\t\t\t\t\t<\/div>\n\t\t\t\t<\/div>\n\t\t\t\t
